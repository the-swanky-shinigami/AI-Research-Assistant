# Architect

This is a sophisticated challenge because contrastive learning (CL) typically relies on continuous data augmentations (like rotating an image), whereas SNPs are discrete, categorical, and biologically constrained. 

To design a robust model for SNP classification, we must move beyond simple data augmentation and instead focus on **semantic consistency**—ensuring that the model learns biological representations rather than noise.

---

### 1. Research Objective
The goal is to learn a latent representation space where SNPs with similar functional impacts or genetic associations are clustered together, while dissimilar SNPs are pushed apart. This pre-trained representation will then be used for downstream classification (e.g., Pathogenic vs. Benign).

### 2. Proposed Architecture: "SNP-ContrastNet"

I propose a **Multi-View Hybrid Encoder** architecture using a **Supervised Contrastive Learning (SupCon)** framework if labels are available, or a **Self-Supervised Masked-Genomic approach** if they are not.

#### A. The Input Pipeline
Since a single SNP is just one point, the model must ingest the **SNP Context Window**:
1.  **Primary Sequence View:** The raw nucleotides surrounding the SNP (e.g., 100bp upstream/downstream).
2.  **Functional Annotation View:** A vector of continuous scores (CADD, PhyloP, GERP++) and categorical flags (Exonic, Intronic) associated with that position.

#### B. The Encoder Architecture
I recommend a **Dual-Stream Encoder**:
*   **Sequence Stream:** A 1D-CNN or a lightweight Transformer (Attention mechanism) to capture local motifs and long-range dependencies in the DNA sequence.
*   **Annotation Stream:** A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to project high-dimensional functional metadata into a dense vector.
*   **Fusion Layer:** A cross-attention module that weighs the importance of the sequence features based on the functional annotations.

#### C. The Projection Head
Following the SimCLR paradigm, the fused embedding $\mathbf{z}$ is passed through a non-linear projection head (MLP with ReLU) to map it to a hypersphere where the contrastive loss is calculated. This prevents the encoder from "collapsing" and losing information necessary for the final classification task.

---

### 3. Algorithmic Design: Defining "Positive Pairs"

The core of CL is the definition of a positive pair $(x_i, x_j)$. For SNPs, I propose three potential strategies depending on the data availability:

#### Strategy 1: Stochastic Genomic Masking (Self-Supervised)
*   **View A:** The original SNP context.
*   **View B:** The same context but with random "dropout" of non-target nucleotides or synthetic noise injected into the functional scores.
*   **Goal:** Force the model to recognize the identity of the SNP despite local sequence perturbations.

#### Strategy 2: Linkage Disequilibrium (LD) Pairing (Biologically Informed)
*   **View A:** Target SNP $S_1$.
*   **View B:** An SNP $S_2$ that is in high LD ($r^2 > 0.8$) with $S_1$.
*   **Goal:** Since SNPs in high LD are inherited together and often share functional associations, they should reside close to each other in the latent space.

#### Strategy 3: Label-Aware Contrast (Supervised)
*   **View A:** Any SNP labeled "Pathogenic."
*   **View B:** Any *other* SNP also labeled "Pathogenic."
*   **Goal:** Maximize intra-class similarity and inter-class distance explicitly.

---

### 4. Comparison of Approaches

| Approach | Data Requirement | Biological Intuition | Risk |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Masking (SimCLR style)** | Unlabeled data | Robustness to noise | May learn trivial patterns; ignores biology. |
| **LD-Pairing** | Genomic Map/Phasing | Co-inheritance logic | High computational cost to build LD graphs. |
| **Supervised Contrast** | Labeled data | Direct functional mapping | Risk of overfitting to small labeled sets. |

---

### 5. Trade-offs and Weaknesses

#### Trade-off: Sequence vs. Annotation
*   If we rely too heavily on the **Sequence Stream**, the model may struggle with "dark matter" regions where sequence alone doesn't imply function.
*   If we rely too heavily on the **Annotation Stream**, the model becomes a wrapper for existing tools (like CADD) and fails to discover *new* biological patterns.

#### Weaknesses:
1.  **The Discrete Bottleneck:** Unlike images, you cannot "slightly shift" a nucleotide. Any change is a discrete jump, which can make the loss landscape jagged.
2.  **Class Imbalance:** Rare variants (the most critical for classification) will be under-represented in the contrastive batches, potentially leading the model to ignore them as outliers.
3.  **Context Window Size:** Choosing too small a window misses distal enhancers; choosing too large a window introduces noise and increases memory overhead.

### 6. Final Workflow Summary
1.  **Pre-train:** Use **LD-Pairing** (Strategy 2) with the **Dual-Stream Encoder** to learn genomic representations from millions of unlabeled SNPs.
2.  **Loss Function:** Optimize using **NT-Xent (Normalized Temperature-scaled Cross Entropy)** loss.
3.  **Downstream Task:** Freeze the encoder, discard the projection head, and attach a Softmax classifier for the specific SNP classification goal.
