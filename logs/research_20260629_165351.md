# Architecture Design: Contrastive Learning for SNP Classification

## 1. Research Objective
The goal is to design a self-supervised representation learning framework that leverages large volumes of unlabeled genomic data to improve the classification of Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs). 

Given that labeled functional annotations for SNPs are scarce compared to the abundance of raw sequence data, this architecture employs **Contrastive Learning**. The objective is to map genomic sequences containing variants into a latent space where SNPs with similar biological effects are clustered together, and those with divergent effects are pushed apart, thereby improving downstream classification accuracy (e.g., pathogenic vs. benign).

---

## 2. Proposed Architecture: SNP-ContrasT (SNP Contrastive Transformer)

The architecture follows a two-stage pipeline: **Self-Supervised Pre-training** (Contrastive Phase) and **Supervised Fine-tuning** (Classification Phase).

### 2.1 Encoder Design
I propose a **Hybrid CNN-Transformer Encoder**. 
*   **Local Feature Extractor (CNN):** A series of 1D convolutional layers to capture local k-mer motifs and short-range dependencies (e.g., transcription factor binding sites).
*   **Global Contextualizer (Transformer):** A Transformer encoder block with multi-head self-attention to capture long-range interactions between the SNP site and distant enhancers or promoters.

### 2.2 Contrastive Learning Strategy
We utilize a **Variant-Aware Augmented Framework**. Instead of generic image-style augmentations, we use biologically informed perturbations.

**Positive Pair Generation $\mathcal{P}(x, x^+)$:**
1.  **Sequence Jittering:** Small, non-functional mutations in the flanking regions of the SNP.
2.  **Contextual Cropping:** Randomly shifting the window size around the SNP while keeping the variant centered.
3.  **Synonymous Substitution:** For coding regions, replacing a base with another that preserves the amino acid.

**Negative Pair Generation $\mathcal{N}(x, x^-)$:**
1.  **Random Sampling:** Sequences from different chromosomes or distant genomic loci.
2.  **Hard Negative Mining:** Selecting SNPs that are sequence-similar but have known opposite functional labels (if a small subset of labels is available during pre-training).

### 2.3 The Contrastive Objective
The model employs the **NT-Xent (Normalized Temperature-scaled Cross Entropy) loss**, treating the problem as a series of binary classifications to identify the positive pair among a set of noise negatives:

$$\mathcal{L}_{i,j} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_j)/\tau)}{\sum_{k=1}^{2N} \mathbb{1}_{[k \neq i]} \exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_k)/\tau)}$$

Where $z$ is the output of a non-linear **Projection Head** (MLP) that maps the encoder's latent space to a hypersphere.

---

## 3. Algorithm Workflow

### Phase I: Self-Supervised Representation Learning
1.  **Input:** A window of DNA sequence centered on an SNP.
2.  **Augmentation:** Generate two views ($x_i, x_j$) using the biological augmentation strategies.
3.  **Encoding:** Pass both views through the CNN-Transformer $\rightarrow$ Latent Vector $h$.
4.  **Projection:** Map $h \rightarrow z$ via the MLP Projection Head.
5.  **Optimization:** Minimize NT-Xent loss to maximize agreement between $z_i$ and $z_j$.

### Phase II: Downstream Classification
1.  **Architecture Shift:** Discard the Projection Head.
2.  **Classifier Attachment:** Append a Softmax layer (or a Random Forest/SVM probe) to the frozen or trainable Encoder.
3.  **Fine-tuning:** Train on labeled SNP data (e.g., ClinVar, gnomAD) using Cross-Entropy loss.

---

## 4. Comparison of Approaches

| Approach | Mechanism | Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Supervised Only** | Direct mapping $\text{Seq} \rightarrow \text{Label}$ | Simple, direct objective. | Requires massive labeled data; prone to overfitting. |
| **Masked LM (BERT)** | Predicting masked bases | Learns general DNA grammar well. | Doesn't explicitly learn "similarity" between variant effects. |
| **Contrastive (Proposed)** | Maximizing similarity of views | Robust features; works with unlabeled data; learns invariant representations. | High computational cost for negative sampling. |

---

## 5. Trade-offs and Design Decisions

### 5.1 CNN vs. Pure Transformer
*   **Decision:** Hybrid approach.
*   **Trade-off:** A pure Transformer has $O(n^2)$ complexity and struggles with the local "motif" nature of DNA. Adding a CNN front-end reduces the sequence length entering the Transformer, decreasing compute while preserving high-frequency local patterns.

### 5.2 Projection Head: To Keep or Discard?
*   **Decision:** Use an MLP projection head during pre-training and discard it for classification.
*   **Trade-off:** Research indicates that the contrastive loss collapses information to satisfy the objective. The projection head acts as a "buffer," allowing the encoder $h$ to retain more general features useful for downstream tasks than the contrastive vector $z$.

### 5.3 Batch Size vs. Memory
*   **Decision:** Use Large-batch training or a Memory Bank/MoCo queue.
*   **Trade-off:** Contrastive learning relies heavily on the number of negative samples. Increasing batch size improves gradient stability but exhausts GPU VRAM. A queue system allows for thousands of negatives without proportional memory growth.

---

## 6. Potential Weaknesses & Mitigations

1.  **Biological Noise:** Some "positive" augmentations (like jittering) might accidentally alter a critical regulatory motif, creating a false positive.
    *   *Mitigation:* Implement **Conservation-Aware Augmentation**, where bases with high evolutionary conservation scores (e.g., PhyloP) are protected from augmentation.
2.  **Class Imbalance:** Benign SNPs vastly outnumber pathogenic ones in nature.
    *   *Mitigation:* Use a weighted loss function during the fine-tuning phase or employ synthetic oversampling (SMOTE) in the latent space.
3.  **Sequence Homology:** Highly similar sequences from gene duplications might be treated as "positives" even if their functional impact differs.
    *   *Mitigation:* Incorporate genomic coordinates as a positional encoding to distinguish between paralogous regions.
