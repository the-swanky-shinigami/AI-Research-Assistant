# Architect

To design a contrastive learning model for Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) classification, we must address the primary challenge of genomic data: **the extreme imbalance between the abundance of raw sequence data and the scarcity of high-quality functional labels** (e.g., pathogenic vs. benign).

The goal is to learn a representation space where SNPs with similar biological effects are clustered together, regardless of their specific genomic coordinates.

---

### 1. Research Objective
Develop a self-supervised representation learning framework that leverages large-scale unlabeled genomic sequences to pre-train an encoder. This encoder will then be fine-tuned for the classification of SNP functional impacts using a small set of labeled data.

### 2. Proposed Architecture: "SNP-ContrastNet"

I propose a **Hybrid CNN-Transformer Encoder** utilizing a **Supervised Contrastive Learning (SupCon)** objective.

#### A. The Input Pipeline
Instead of treating an SNP as a single point, we treat it as a **localized genomic window**.
*   **Input:** A fixed-length sequence centered on the SNP (e.g., 1kb flanking regions).
*   **Encoding:** One-hot encoding of nucleotides $\{A, C, G, T\}$.

#### B. The Encoder Architecture ($\text{Enc}_\theta$)
I recommend a two-stage feature extraction process:
1.  **Local Motif Extractor (CNN):** A series of 1D convolutional layers with varying filter sizes (e.g., 3, 7, 15). This mimics Position Weight Matrices (PWMs) to detect transcription factor binding sites or splice motifs affected by the SNP.
2.  **Global Contextualizer (Transformer/Mamba):** A lightweight Transformer encoder or a State Space Model (SSM like Mamba) to capture long-range dependencies and distal regulatory elements that might interact with the SNP site.

#### C. The Projection Head ($g_\phi$)
Following the SimCLR paradigm, the output of the encoder is passed through a non-linear MLP projection head. This maps the high-dimensional genomic embedding into a latent space where contrastive loss is calculated. The projection head is discarded during the downstream classification phase.

---

### 3. Contrastive Learning Strategy

The core mechanism is to define what constitutes "similarity" in genomics.

#### Data Augmentation (Creating Positive Pairs)
Since we cannot "rotate" or "flip" DNA like an image, we use biological augmentations:
*   **Reverse Complement (RC):** The RC of a sequence is biologically equivalent. An SNP and its RC version are the ultimate positive pair.
*   **Stochastic Masking:** Randomly masking non-SNP positions in the flanking region to force the model to rely on the SNP site and surrounding motifs rather than specific noise.
*   **Jittering:** Slightly shifting the window center (if the SNP is part of a cluster).

#### The Contrastive Objective
I propose using **Supervised Contrastive Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{SupCon}$)** if partial labels are available, or **InfoNCE** for purely unsupervised pre-training.

*   **Positive Pairs:** $(x_i, x_{i^+})$ — Two views of the same SNP, or two different SNPs known to have the same functional effect (e.g., both cause loss-of-function in the same gene).
*   **Negative Pairs:** $(x_i, x_j)$ — SNPs with different effects or SNPs located in unrelated genomic regions.

---

### 4. Comparison of Approaches

| Approach | Mechanism | Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **SimCLR (Unsupervised)** | Contrast views of the same sequence. | No labels needed; massive scale. | May learn "sequence identity" rather than "biological function." |
| **MoCo (Momentum)** | Uses a queue for negative samples. | Memory efficient; larger batch sizes. | Higher architectural complexity. |
| **SupCon (Supervised)** | Pulls all SNPs of the same class together. | Much stronger signals for classification. | Requires some labeled data for pre-training. |
| **BYOL (Bootstrapping)** | No negative pairs; predicts one view from another. | Avoids "collapse" without negatives. | Slower convergence in genomic tasks. |

**Architect's Choice:** I recommend **SupCon**. In SNP classification, the difference between a benign and pathogenic variant is often a single nucleotide; unsupervised methods may ignore this tiny delta as "noise." SupCon forces the model to recognize that two different sequences with the same pathology are functionally identical.

---

### 5. Identified Weaknesses & Mitigation

**Weakness 1: The "Position Bias" Problem.**
The model might learn to classify SNPs based on *where* they are in the genome (coordinate-based) rather than *what* they do (motif-based).
*   *Mitigation:* Use **Coordinate Shuffling**. During training, ensure that positive pairs consist of SNPs from different chromosomes/regions that share the same functional label.

**Weakness 2: Sequence Homology.**
High sequence similarity between non-functional regions can lead to "false positives" in contrastive learning.
*   *Mitigation:* Implement **Hard Negative Mining**. Specifically select negative samples that are sequence-similar but functionally different (e.g., a benign SNP and a pathogenic SNP in the same gene).

**Weakness 3: Computational Complexity.**
Transformers on long genomic windows are $O(n^2)$.
*   *Mitigation:* Use **Linear Attention** or **Mamba blocks** to reduce complexity to $O(n)$, allowing for larger flanking windows (e.g., moving from 1kb to 10kb).

---

### 6. Summary of Trade-offs

1.  **Window Size vs. Resolution:** Larger windows capture distal enhancers but introduce noise and computational overhead. I propose a **multi-scale approach**: a narrow window for the SNP site and a dilated wide window for context.
2.  **CNN vs. Transformer:** CNNs are better at motif detection; Transformers are better at syntax/grammar. A hybrid ensures we don't miss the "needle" (the SNP) while understanding the "haystack" (the genome).
3.  **Self-Supervised vs. Supervised:** Pure self-supervision is scalable but biologically agnostic. Adding a supervised contrastive layer bridges the gap between sequence patterns and clinical phenotypes.
