# Architect

This is a complex architectural challenge because genomic data—specifically Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs)—differs fundamentally from images or text. SNPs are discrete, high-dimensional, sparse, and exhibit strong local correlations known as Linkage Disequilibrium (LD).

To design a contrastive learning model for SNP classification, we must move beyond simple data augmentation and instead focus on **biological inductive biases**.

---

### 1. Research Objective
The goal is to learn a robust, low-dimensional representation of genomic profiles such that samples with similar phenotypic expressions are clustered together in the latent space, while dissimilar ones are pushed apart. This overcomes the "curse of dimensionality" ($p \gg n$) typically found in Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS).

### 2. Proposed Architecture: "SNP-ContrastNet"

I propose a **Self-Supervised Pre-training + Supervised Fine-tuning** pipeline using a Siamese Network architecture.

#### A. The Encoder ($\text{Enc}_\theta$)
Given the nature of SNPs, a hybrid encoder is required:
1.  **Local Feature Extractor (1D-CNN):** To capture Linkage Disequilibrium (LD). Nearby SNPs are often inherited together; 1D convolutions act as "LD-filters" to extract local haplotype patterns.
2.  **Global Context Aggregator (Transformer Encoder):** To capture long-range epistatic interactions (interactions between distant SNPs on different chromosomes) via Multi-Head Self-Attention.
3.  **Pooling Layer:** Global Average Pooling to collapse the sequence into a fixed-length embedding vector $\mathbf{z}$.

#### B. The Projection Head ($\text{Proj}_\phi$)
A small MLP (Multi-Layer Perceptron) that maps the representation $\mathbf{z}$ to a hypersphere where the contrastive loss is calculated. This prevents the encoder from collapsing and preserves information for the downstream classification task.

---

### 3. Algorithm Design: The Contrastive Strategy

Since we cannot "rotate" or "crop" an SNP profile like an image, we must define **Genomic Augmentations** to create positive pairs $(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_i')$.

#### Step 1: Genomic Data Augmentation ($\mathcal{T}$)
To generate a synthetic positive pair from a single sample $\mathbf{x}$:
*   **Feature Masking:** Randomly mask $15\%$ of SNPs (replace with a "mask" token). This forces the model to reconstruct missing genomic information from surrounding LD blocks.
*   **LD-Aware Jittering:** Introduce noise only to SNPs with low Minor Allele Frequency (MAF), preserving the high-impact variants.
*   **Haplotype Shuffling:** Swap segments of SNPs within known non-coding regions to ensure the model focuses on functional variations.

#### Step 2: The Contrastive Objective
We utilize the **NT-Xent (Normalized Temperature-scaled Cross Entropy)** loss. For a batch of $N$ samples, we create $2N$ augmented views. The goal is to maximize the similarity between two views of the same individual and minimize it against all others in the batch.

$$\mathcal{L}_{i,j} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{z}_j) / \tau)}{\sum_{k=1}^{2N} \mathbb{1}_{[k \neq i]} \exp(\text{sim}(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{z}_k) / \tau)}$$

#### Step 3: Downstream Classification
After pre-training the encoder on a large unlabeled genomic dataset, we discard the projection head and attach a linear classifier (Softmax layer). We fine-tune the entire network using labeled phenotypic data.

---

### 4. Comparison of Approaches

| Approach | Mechanism | Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Standard Supervised** | Direct $\text{SNP} \rightarrow \text{Label}$ | Simple to implement. | Prone to overfitting due to $p \gg n$. |
| **Autoencoder (AE)** | Reconstruction loss | Learns compressed representation. | Focuses on reconstructing noise; ignores class boundaries. |
| **Contrastive (Proposed)** | Instance Discrimination | Learns robust, invariant features; leverages unlabeled data. | Requires careful design of augmentations to avoid destroying signal. |

---

### 5. Trade-offs and Weaknesses

#### Trade-offs:
*   **Computational Complexity vs. Resolution:** Using a Transformer allows for global interaction capture but increases memory complexity quadratically $O(L^2)$ relative to the number of SNPs ($L$). We must use **Windowed Attention** or **Linear Transformers** if the SNP set is massive.
*   **Augmentation Strength:** If masking is too aggressive, we lose the causal variant; if it's too light, the model learns a trivial identity function.

#### Identified Weaknesses:
1.  **The "False Negative" Problem:** In contrastive learning, two different individuals with the same phenotype are treated as "negative pairs." This can confuse the latent space. 
    *   *Mitigation:* Transition to **Supervised Contrastive Learning (SupCon)** if a portion of labels is available during pre-training.
2.  **Sparsity:** SNPs are often encoded as $\{0, 1, 2\}$. Standard embeddings may struggle with the discrete nature of this data compared to continuous signals.
3.  **LD Leakage:** If augmentations do not respect LD blocks, the model might learn to identify individuals based on noise rather than biological signal.

### Summary of Architecture Flow
$\text{Input SNPs} \rightarrow \text{Augmentation}(\mathcal{T}_1, \mathcal{T}_2) \rightarrow \text{1D-CNN} \rightarrow \text{Transformer} \rightarrow \text{MLP Head} \rightarrow \text{NT-Xent Loss} \rightarrow \text{Fine-tune for Classification}$.
